KYC Regulations ▼
KYC (Know Your Customer) regulations are essential components of anti-money laundering (AML) compliance frameworks globally. In the U.S., KYC requirements are primarily governed by the Bank Secrecy Act (BSA), as implemented and enforced by the Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN). These regulations ensure that financial institutions verify and understand their customers to prevent financial crimes, such as money laundering, terrorist financing, and fraud.

Key Components of KYC Regulations
  • 1. Customer Identification Program (CIP):
    • • Financial institutions must implement a CIP to verify the identity of individuals or entities before opening an account.
    • • Minimum information collected:
      • • Name
      • • Date of birth (for individuals)
      • • Address
      • • Identification number (e.g., Social Security Number for U.S. residents or a Taxpayer Identification Number for non-residents).
    • • Verification must include:
      • • Documentary methods (e.g., government-issued ID).
      • • Non-documentary methods (e.g., credit reports or database checks).
  • 2. Customer Due Diligence (CDD):
    • • Institutions must gather information to assess and verify the customer’s risk profile and expected activity.
    • • Key activities:
      • • Understand the purpose and nature of the customer relationship.
      • • Identify the beneficial owners of legal entity customers.
      • • Monitor transactions for consistency with the customer’s stated purposes.
  • 3. Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD):
    • • For high-risk customers, such as politically exposed persons (PEPs) or those in high-risk jurisdictions, additional verification and monitoring are required.
  • 4. Ongoing Monitoring and Updates:
    • • Institutions must continuously monitor customer activity and periodically update records to identify changes in risk.
  • 5. Recordkeeping Requirements:
    • • All collected KYC data must be retained for at least 5 years after the account is closed.


Relevant Regulations and Guidelines

  • • 31 CFR Part 1010 (General Provisions for Financial Institutions).
  • • FinCEN Final Rule on CDD Requirements (May 2016): Expanded obligations to identify and verify the beneficial owners of legal entities.
  • • USA PATRIOT Act, Section 326: Requires financial institutions to establish and maintain CIPs.

How to Implement KYC Compliance
  • 1. Use automated KYC solutions to verify identities efficiently.
  • 2. Conduct risk assessments based on customer profiles and activities.
  • 3. Establish policies and train employees to identify suspicious behavior.
  • 4. Regularly audit and update KYC processes to ensure compliance with evolving regulations.

U.S. Insider Trading Regulations ▼
U.S. insider trading regulations are governed by federal securities laws, particularly the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, and enforced by the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC).

These regulations aim to maintain market integrity by prohibiting individuals with material, nonpublic information from trading securities or share this information with others for personal gain.

Key Components of Insider Trading Regulations

  • 1. Definition of Insider Trading: Insider trading occurs when individuals trade securities based on material, nonpublic information. This includes:
    • • Classical Insider Trading: Executed by corporate insiders, such as executives or directors, who owe a fiduciary duty to shareholders.
    • • Misappropriation Theory: Involves individuals who improperly obtain confidential information and trade on it.
  • 2. Material, Nonpublic Information:
    • • Material Information: Information that a reasonable investor would consider important in making investment decisions (e.g., earnings reports, mergers, or acquisitions).
    • • Nonpublic Information: Information not yet disclosed to the public or widely disseminated.
  • 3. Prohibited Activities:
    • • Buying or selling securities based on insider information.
    • • Tipping or sharing insider information with others who then trade on it (tipper-tippee liability).
    • • Trading securities during blackout periods imposed by the company.
  • 4. Disclosure Obligations:
    • • Corporate insiders must report trades under Section 16(a) of the Securities Exchange Act.
    • • Certain large trades or beneficial ownership changes must be disclosed through Form 4 filings with the SEC.
  • 5. Penalties for Violations:
    • • Civil Penalties: Fines of up to three times the profit gained or loss avoided.
    • • Criminal Penalties: Up to 20 years in prison and fines up to $5 million for individuals and $25 million for entities.
    • • Reputational damage and potential loss of professional licenses.


Key Laws and Rules Governing Insider Trading :

  • 1. Securities Exchange Act of 1934:
    • • Section 10(b): Prohibits fraudulent activities related to securities trading.
    • • Rule 10b-5: Broadly prohibits deceptive practices in connection with the purchase or sale of securities.
  • 2. Insider Trading Sanctions Act of 1984:
    • • Imposes civil penalties on violators and tippees.
  • 3. Insider Trading and Securities Fraud Enforcement Act of 1988:
    • • Strengthens penalties and requires firms to adopt policies to prevent insider trading.
  • 4. Regulation FD (Fair Disclosure):
    • • Ensures that material information is disclosed to all investors simultaneously to prevent selective disclosure.


Preventing Insider Trading

  • 1. Compliance Programs:
    • • Firms must establish robust insider trading policies, including trading windows and blackout periods.
    • • Provide regular training to employees on insider trading laws.
  • 2. Monitoring and Reporting:
    • • Use surveillance systems to monitor trades.
    • • Require pre-clearance for certain transactions.
  • 3. Whistleblower Protections:
    • • Encourage employees to report violations through internal mechanisms.
    • • Protect whistleblowers under the Dodd-Frank Act.


Additional Resources

  • 2. Information on reporting requirements: SEC Forms and Filings.

2. Introduction
  • 2.1 What is money laundering?

    Money laundering is the process of hiding or disguising the source of illegally obtained "dirty" funds to make them appear legal and "clean". Money laundering reduces the risk of detection and seizure by authorities, and money laundering is as serious as the criminal activities behind it. Preventing money laundering helps reduce crime.
  • 2.2 What is terrorist financing?

    Terrorist financing differs from money laundering in three main ways:
    • 1) Its main purpose is to conceal the final use of funds, not the source of funds;
    • 2) It may involve relatively small amounts of funds, but may cause death, injury and other damage with greater impact;
    • 3) Although terrorists may finance their activities through crime, legitimate funds may also be diverted to finance terrorism.
  • 2.3 What is suspicion?

    Money laundering and terrorist financing ("ML/TF") are sometimes discovered because of suspicious or suspicious behavior of members. In order for "suspicion" to be established, the website must have reasonable grounds to believe that money laundering and terrorist financing activities may be occurring. To support this, staff are trained to identify and report suspicious matters.
